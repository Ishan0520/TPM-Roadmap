# 📖 Overview

Samsung Electronics is one of the most complex technology companies globally, operating across:

- Semiconductor memory (DRAM, NAND)
- Logic & SoC design (Exynos, AI chips)
- Foundry services (contract manufacturing)
- Displays (OLED)
- Consumer devices (smartphones, TVs, appliances)

What makes Samsung unique is that it is **simultaneously a component supplier, manufacturer, and end-product company**.
This creates a **closed-loop ecosystem**, where Samsung designs chips, manufactures them, and deploys them inside its own products—while also serving external customers.

# 🚀 Why Samsung

What makes Samsung unique is not just its scale, but how deeply integrated its operations are across the entire technology stack.

Unlike most companies that specialize in a single layer of the ecosystem, Samsung operates simultaneously as a **component supplier, manufacturer, and product company**. It designs memory and logic chips, manufactures them in its own fabs, and deploys them directly into its own devices while also supplying external customers. This creates a powerful internal feedback loop between design, manufacturing, and real-world product performance.

What stands out even more is Samsung’s ability to balance two fundamentally different businesses: **memory (high-volume, cyclical, cost-driven)** and **foundry/logic (precision-driven, customer-centric, and yield-sensitive)**. Managing both at scale requires a level of operational discipline and coordination that very few companies in the world can achieve.

I’m particularly interested in how Samsung is positioning itself in the AI era. The combination of high-bandwidth memory, advanced process nodes, and system-level integration places Samsung at a critical point in the AI hardware stack. Success in this space depends not just on innovation, but on execution—yield, supply chain coordination, and large-scale manufacturing reliability.

For me, Samsung represents an opportunity to work at the intersection of **engineering, manufacturing, and global supply chain systems**, where decisions directly impact multi-billion-dollar programs and the future of computing infrastructure.
